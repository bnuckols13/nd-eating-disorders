Understanding the Connection
Why Food is Hard for ND Brains

It's not about willpower

ND brains — whether ADHD, autistic, or both — often have a different relationship with interoception, the body's ability to sense internal signals like hunger, fullness, and thirst. When interoception is muted or inconsistent, eating "normally" is genuinely harder.

Add sensory sensitivities, executive function challenges, and emotional dysregulation into the mix, and food becomes a complicated daily negotiation — not a simple choice.

Something to sit with: Research shows autistic people are significantly more likely to experience ARFID, anorexia, and binge eating. ADHD is strongly linked to binge eating and restriction cycles. This isn't coincidence — it's neurobiology.

The shame spiral that makes it worse

When eating feels chaotic, we often blame ourselves. But the shame spiral — "I should be able to do this basic thing" — creates more nervous system dysregulation, which makes eating even harder. It's a loop, not a character flaw.

What Actually Helps
Practical Strategies for ND Brains
⏰

Timed reminders instead of hunger cues

If your hunger signals are unreliable, external cues can substitute. Phone alarms, watch vibrations, or a "eating anchor" tied to an existing routine (after your morning meds, at the halfway point of your workday).

🧩

Safe foods are not failures

Having go-to foods that feel safe, predictable, and low-effort is adaptive — not avoidance. Building a personal "safe food list" you can return to on hard days is a valid strategy, full stop.

🧠

Lower the decision load

Decision fatigue hits ND brains hard. Reducing choices — same breakfast rotation, pre-decided snacks, a short list of "good enough" meals — preserves energy for things that matter more to you.

🎧

Pair eating with something regulating

Eating alone in silence can feel activating or dissociating. Pairing meals with a podcast, show, music, or background noise can lower the threshold to actually eat.

🌡️

Check in with your body differently

Instead of "am I hungry?" try "am I low energy, irritable, or having trouble concentrating?" — these are often hunger signals ND bodies translate differently.

💛

Enough is a moving target

Some days "enough" is a full meal. Some days it's crackers and cheese at 10pm. Reducing the gap between "what I ate" and "what I think I should have eaten" is progress.

"Taking care of your body in a way that works for your brain is not settling. It is creativity. It is survival. It is worth celebrating."

The Planning Problem
Executive Function & Food

When the fridge feels impossible

Executive function challenges — planning, initiating, sequencing — make cooking genuinely hard, not just inconvenient. Standing in front of an open fridge unable to decide or start is a real executive function moment, not laziness.

📋

The "body doubling" approach

Eating with someone else (virtually or in person), even without talking, can help with initiation. Body doubling activates different brain pathways for ND folks.

🛒

Shop for your worst-day self

Stocking your kitchen for the version of you who is depleted, dysregulated, and can't cook — not the aspirational version — means you're more likely to eat something when it counts.

🔁

Rituals over routines

Routines can feel rigid and triggering. Rituals feel intentional and personal. Reframing — "I always have tea when I sit down to eat" — can make the same behavior feel more workable.
