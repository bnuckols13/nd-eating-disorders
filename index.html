A Space That Knows Your Brain

You don't have to explain yourself here — or perform okay-ness around food. Both are allowed to be complicated.

Today we're not talking about rules, meal plans, or the right way to eat. We're talking about what actually helps — the real, sometimes messy, creative workarounds ND people find. Because what works for neurotypical brains often doesn't work for ours, and that is not a personal failure.

This group is for ADHD, autism, OCD, dyspraxia, sensory processing differences, dyscalculia, dyslexia — and any brain that has ever found the standard food advice completely useless. That's neuroscience, not personal failing.

Why Food is Hard for ND Brains

10 min

ND brains often have a different relationship with interoception: the body's ability to sense internal signals like hunger, fullness, and thirst. When interoception is muted, delayed, or unreliable, eating "normally" is genuinely harder. Not because of character. Because of wiring.

Add sensory sensitivities, executive function challenges that make cooking feel impossible, and emotional dysregulation that drowns out hunger and fullness — and food becomes a complicated daily negotiation, not a simple choice.

This Group Is for You If...

  • ADHD — binge-restrict cycles, impulsivity, dopamine-seeking with food, hyperfocus that skips meals
  • Autism — ARFID, sensory sensitivities, rigidity around food rules, mealtime anxiety
  • OCD — contamination fears, rigid food rituals, restriction driven by intrusive thoughts
  • Dyspraxia / DCD — motor planning challenges that make cooking initiation genuinely hard
  • Sensory Processing Differences — textures, temperatures, smells, sounds that make eating activating
  • Dyscalculia or Dyslexia — added cognitive load around recipes, labels, portion math
  • Any ND brain that experiences food as a daily negotiation rather than a simple act
Research Note

Eating disorder rates are significantly elevated across ND profiles. Autistic people are up to five times more likely to experience ARFID or anorexia. ADHD is strongly linked to binge-restrict cycles. OCD is independently associated with restrictive eating and food-related rituals. Dyspraxia raises cooking initiation barriers. None of this is coincidence — it's converging neurobiology.

The Shame Loop

When eating feels chaotic, we often blame ourselves: "I should be able to do this basic human thing." But the shame spiral creates more nervous system dysregulation — which makes eating even harder. It's a loop, not a character flaw.

What the Loop Says

"I should be able to handle this. Normal people eat. Why is this so hard for me?"

What's Actually True

Your nervous system is working exactly as it was built to. The loop is real — and it's not about willpower.

Pause here. Ask: "Does any of this sound familiar — even a little bit?" You don't need to fix the loop today. Naming it is already something. Give the room a genuine moment of quiet before continuing.

Naming the loop is not the same as being stuck in it. It is the beginning of something different.

Practical Strategies for ND Brains

15 min

These aren't rules. They're starting points — things other ND people have found actually useful. Take what fits. Leave the rest.

⏰

External Hunger Cues

If your internal hunger signals are unreliable, external cues can substitute. Phone alarms, watch vibrations, or anchoring eating to an existing routine — after morning meds, at the midpoint of the workday — instead of waiting to feel hungry.
Why This WorksInteroceptive accuracy is measurably reduced in ADHD, autism, OCD, and dyspraxia. Garfinkel et al. (2016) found autistic people often can't reliably detect hunger until it's extreme. External cues bypass this gap entirely.
🧩

Safe Foods Are Not Failures

Having go-to foods that feel predictable and sensory-okay is adaptive, not avoidance. A personal "safe food list" you return to on hard days is a valid strategy — full stop.
Why This WorksARFID literature (Koomar et al., 2021) and sensory integration research (Cermak et al., 2010) show predictable sensory input reduces anticipatory anxiety and decision load at once. That's a real dual mechanism, not a shortcut.
🧠

Reduce the Decision Load

Decision fatigue hits ND brains hard. A rotating breakfast, pre-decided snacks, a short "good enough meals" list — reducing choices preserves energy for things that matter more to you.
Why This WorksBaumeister et al.'s decision fatigue research shows each choice depletes the same prefrontal resources used for emotional regulation. For ND brains already working harder at baseline, fewer food decisions means more capacity for everything else.
🎧

Pair Eating with Regulation

Eating in silence can feel activating or dissociating for ND people. Pairing meals with a podcast, show, or background noise can meaningfully lower the threshold to actually eat.
Why This WorksSensory input modulates arousal through the reticular activating system. The sensory diet framework (Ayres, 1972; Kranowitz, 2005) uses background stimulation to bring the nervous system to a regulated state — which directly lowers the threshold for starting a meal.
🌡️

Translate Hunger Differently

Instead of "am I hungry?" try: "am I low energy, irritable, losing focus?" These are often how hunger shows up in ND bodies — the body speaking a different dialect.
Why This WorksAlexithymia — difficulty identifying internal states — is present in ~50% of autistic people and elevated in ADHD (Bird & Cook, 2013). Hunger often registers as mood shift, irritability, or cognitive fog before it registers as physical sensation.
🛒

Shop for Your Worst-Day Self

Stock your kitchen for the version of you who is depleted and can't cook — not the aspirational version. Easy, accessible food on hand means you're more likely to eat something when it counts.
Why This WorksExecutive function research (Barkley, 2011) shows ND brains have the most difficulty initiating tasks under depletion. Pre-stocked options remove the need for planning at exactly the moment those capacities are lowest.
👥

Body Doubling

Eating with someone — virtually or in person, even without talking — can help with initiation. Body doubling activates different neural pathways for many ND people.
Why This WorksSocial presence activates task-initiation networks in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex that many ADHD and autistic brains have difficulty self-starting alone. Brown & Halpern (2021) found this effect holds even in remote or asynchronous settings.
💛

Enough Is a Moving Target

Some days "enough" is a full meal. Some days it's crackers and cheese at 10pm. Narrowing the gap between what you ate and what you think you should have eaten is its own progress.
Why This WorksNeff's self-compassion research (2003) and Porges' polyvagal theory (2011) both show shame triggers a physiological threat response — which directly disrupts eating regulation. Reducing the verdict around one meal lowers activation going into the next.
