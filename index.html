Why Food is Hard for ND Brains

10 min

ND brains — whether ADHD, autistic, or both — often have a different relationship with interoception: the body's ability to sense internal signals like hunger, fullness, and thirst. When interoception is muted, unreliable, or works on a time delay, eating "normally" is genuinely harder. Not because of character. Because of wiring.

Add in sensory sensitivities around food textures and smells, executive function challenges that make cooking feel impossible, and emotional dysregulation that turns hunger and fullness into background noise — and food becomes a complicated daily negotiation, not a simple choice.

Research Note

Studies show autistic people are significantly more likely to experience ARFID, anorexia, and binge eating. ADHD is strongly linked to binge-restrict cycles. This isn't coincidence — it's neurobiology.

✦

The Shame Loop

When eating feels chaotic or out of control, we often blame ourselves. "I should be able to do this basic human thing." But the shame spiral creates more nervous system dysregulation — which makes eating even harder. It's a loop, not a character flaw.

What the Loop Looks Like

  • Can't initiate eating → skip meals → dysregulation spikes
  • Sensory overwhelm → restrict → binge → shame
  • Emotional flooding → eating to regulate → guilt → more flooding
  • Shame about the loop → harder to ask for help

Pause here and invite reflection: "Does any of this sound familiar — even a little bit?" You don't need to fix the loop today. Naming it is already something. Give a moment of quiet before moving on.

Practical Strategies for ND Brains

15 min

These aren't rules. They're starting points — things other ND people have found actually useful. Take what fits. Leave the rest.

⏰

External Hunger Cues

If your hunger signals are unreliable, external cues can substitute. Phone alarms, watch vibrations, or anchoring eating to an existing routine — after morning meds, at the halfway point of the workday — instead of waiting to feel hungry.
🧩

Safe Foods are Not Failures

Having go-to foods that feel predictable, low-effort, and sensory-okay is adaptive, not avoidance. Building a personal "safe food list" you return to on hard days is a valid strategy. Full stop.
🧠

Reduce the Decision Load

Decision fatigue hits ND brains hard. A rotating breakfast, pre-decided snacks, a short "good enough meals" list — reducing choices preserves energy for things that matter more to you.
🎧

Pair Eating with Something Regulating

Eating in silence can feel activating or dissociating for ND people. Pairing meals with a podcast, show, or background noise can meaningfully lower the threshold to actually eat.
🌡️

Translate Hunger Differently

Instead of "am I hungry?" try: "am I low energy, irritable, or losing focus?" These are often how hunger shows up in ND bodies — the body's way of speaking a different dialect.
🛒

Shop for Your Worst-Day Self

Stock your kitchen for the version of you who is depleted and can't cook — not the aspirational version. Keeping easy, accessible food on hand means you're more likely to eat something when it counts.
👥

Body Doubling

Eating with someone — virtually or in person, even without talking — can help with initiation. Body doubling activates different neural pathways for many ND people and makes the whole thing feel more doable.
💛

Enough is a Moving Target

Some days "enough" is a full meal. Some days it's crackers and cheese at 10pm. Narrowing the gap between "what I ate" and "what I think I should have eaten" is its own form of progress.

Taking care of your body in a way that works for your brain is not settling.
It is creativity. It is survival. It is worth recognizing.

Something to Carry With You

A Reminder

You are not failing at eating. You are navigating something genuinely complex with a brain that didn't come with a standard manual. Every small act of nourishment — whatever form it takes — counts.

Between Sessions

  • Notice once this week when hunger shows up as something other than hunger — irritability, brain fog, low energy
  • Identify one "worst-day food" you could keep on hand
  • Be curious instead of critical — even once
